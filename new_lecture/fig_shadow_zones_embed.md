## MyST Markdown — for `chapters/10_whole_earth/lecture_NN.md`

```{figure} ../../assets/figures/fig_shadow_zones.png
:name: fig-shadow-zones
:alt: Two side-by-side schematic Earth cross-sections.  Panel (a):
  S-wave shadow.  Orange S-rays from a star-shaped source at the top
  of the Earth turn back inside the mantle and reach the surface only
  at angular distances less than 103 degrees.  Two dashed orange rays
  that hit the core-mantle boundary terminate at vermilion X markers,
  labelled "S cannot enter fluid outer core (mu = 0)".  The surface
  arc from 103 degrees through the antipode at 180 degrees is shaded
  in vermilion and labelled "S-wave shadow zone".  Panel (b):
  P-wave shadow.  Blue P-rays turn in the mantle and reach the
  surface within 103 degrees; three additional blue rays refract
  sharply into the outer core, traverse it along curved paths that
  skirt the inner core, refract again, and emerge at the surface
  beyond the PKP caustic at 143 degrees.  The middle ray is labelled
  "PKP".  An annotation gives Snell's law at the CMB with mantle and
  outer-core P-wave speeds of 13.7 and 8 km/s.  The surface arc from
  103 degrees to 143 degrees is shaded in vermilion and labelled
  "P-wave shadow zone (PKP fills in beyond 143 degrees)".  Both
  panels use a colorblind-safe palette and label the mantle, outer
  core (fluid, mu = 0), and inner core.
:width: 100%
:align: center

The 103° threshold is geometric — the angular distance at which a direct
mantle ray just grazes the core-mantle boundary. Beyond 103° any direct
ray must penetrate the core. **S-wave energy cannot propagate as a shear
wave in the fluid outer core** ($\mu = 0 \Rightarrow \beta = \sqrt{\mu/\rho} = 0$),
so the S shadow extends from 103° all the way to the antipode. **P-wave
energy refracts into the core** ($\alpha_\text{mantle} \approx 13.7$ km/s,
$\alpha_\text{outer\,core} \approx 8$ km/s; the ray bends toward the
normal by Snell's law) and re-emerges as PKP at distances at or beyond
the PKP caustic near 143°, leaving the 103°–143° gap. The same kinematic
boundary at the CMB combined with one constitutive fact — fluids carry
no shear stress — explains both shadows.
```

> emergence distances and the precise caustic position depend on the
> radial velocity model (PREM, IASP91).

## AI literacy moment (optional, for the lecture's AI box)

> **Prompt Lab — AI Epistemics.** Ask a chat assistant: *"Why does the
> S-wave shadow zone extend to 180° but the P-wave shadow zone only goes
> from 103° to 143°?"* Evaluate the response against this figure. Common
> failure modes: (1) attributing both shadows to "absorption" rather than
> distinguishing fluid/refraction mechanisms; (2) confusing the CMB
> geometry (which sets 103°) with the constitutive property of the fluid
> outer core (which sets the *width*); (3) hallucinating numerical values
> for the velocity contrast. Compare what the assistant says against the
> values in this figure: $\alpha_m \approx 13.7$, $\alpha_c \approx 8$
> km/s; $\beta_c = 0$.
